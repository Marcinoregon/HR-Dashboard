Module 4 · Overview

Correlation vs. Causation

Just because two things move together doesn't mean one causes the other. This is arguably the most important concept in data analysis.

What is Correlation?

Correlation measures the strength and direction of a relationship between two variables. When variable A goes up and variable B also goes up, they are positively correlated. When one goes up and the other goes down, they are negatively correlated.

📊 Dashboard Example In Module 4 (Compensation), you can see that employees with higher prior experience tend to have higher wages. This is a positive correlation. But does experience actually cause better performance?

What is Causation?

Causation means one variable directly influences another. Establishing causation typically requires a controlled experiment or rigorous statistical methods — not just observing patterns in data.

Why It Matters in HR

ObservationCorrelation?Causation?
Higher engagement → Higher profitLikely yesNot proven
More experience → Higher wagesYesWages may reflect tenure, not value
Lower performance → Higher turnoverYesCould be reversed: turnover causes disengagement
Recruitment source → Manager qualityPossiblyOther factors (personality, training) confound
⚠️ Common Trap When the dashboard shows that medium-market units have higher engagement but lower profit, it's tempting to conclude that engagement doesn't matter. But there may be a third variable (e.g., market size, customer volume) driving both. This is called a confounding variable.
If units with higher manager performance ratings have higher profit, can we conclude that better managers cause more profit?
Not necessarily. While better management likely contributes to profit, profitable units may also have stronger support systems, better locations, or higher customer traffic — all of which could make management easier and inflate ratings. To establish causation, we'd need to control for these confounds (e.g., compare managers who moved between low- and high-profit units).

✅ Correct! This is a correlation. Self-selection bias may explain it — healthier employees may be more likely to sign up for wellness programs and have lower absenteeism.
❌ Not quite. We can only say there's a correlation. Self-selection bias is likely at work — employees who care about health may both join wellness programs and have lower absenteeism.

Variance & Comparing Means

An average alone can be misleading. Understanding the spread of your data is just as important as understanding the center.

The Problem with Averages

Imagine two restaurants both have an average performance rating of 3.5. But in restaurant A, all employees scored between 3.0 and 4.0 (low variance). In restaurant B, scores ranged from 1.0 to 5.0 (high variance). These are very different situations that require different interventions.

Standard Deviation

Standard deviation (SD) is the most common measure of spread. It tells you, on average, how far individual data points are from the mean.

Formula SD = √[ Σ(xᵢ - x̄)² / (n - 1) ]
where x̄ = mean, n = sample size, xᵢ = each individual value

Practical Rule (68-95-99.7)

In a normal distribution: 68% of data falls within ±1 SD of the mean, 95% within ±2 SD, and 99.7% within ±3 SD. If a score is more than 2 SDs from the mean, it's statistically unusual.

📊 Dashboard Example When comparing engagement scores between Large and Medium markets (Overview radar chart), the averages may look similar. But if Large-market scores have high variance (some units at 6.0, others at 2.0), a targeted intervention is needed — not a company-wide program.

Why This Matters When Comparing Groups

When the dashboard shows that "Male avg wage = $59,887" and "Female avg wage = $57,515" (Module 4), the difference of ~$2,300 seems meaningful. But is it statistically significant? That depends on sample size (n) and variance (SD). With large SD and small n, a $2,300 gap could easily be random noise.

Two recruitment sources both produce managers with an average performance of 3.8. Source A has 5 managers, Source B has 50. Which average is more trustworthy?
Source B is more trustworthy. With n=50, the sample mean is much more stable (less sampling error). Source A's average of 3.8 could shift dramatically with one additional hire. This is why sample size matters — always check the "n" behind any average on the dashboard.

Adverse Impact & the 4/5ths Rule

Federal guidelines require employers to ensure their selection procedures don't disproportionately screen out members of a protected group. The 4/5ths rule is the primary screening test.

What is Adverse Impact?

Adverse impact (also called disparate impact) occurs when a seemingly neutral employment practice disproportionately affects a protected group. Unlike intentional discrimination (disparate treatment), adverse impact can be unintentional.

The 4/5ths Rule

The Uniform Guidelines on Employee Selection Procedures (1978) provide a practical test:

Impact Ratio Formula Impact Ratio = Selection Rate (minority group) / Selection Rate (majority group)
If the ratio is below 0.80 (4/5ths), adverse impact may exist

What Does This Mean?

An impact ratio of 0.473 is well below the 0.80 threshold. This means URM candidates are being hired at roughly half the rate of White candidates. Possible explanations include:

  • Interview procedures may contain bias (conscious or unconscious)
  • Job-irrelevant criteria may be filtering out qualified URM candidates
  • The applicant pools may differ in relevant qualifications
  • Structural barriers in the recruitment process
⚠️ Legal Implications The 4/5ths rule is a screening test, not a definitive legal standard. If adverse impact is found, the employer must show the selection procedure is job-related and consistent with business necessity. Even then, the plaintiff can prevail by demonstrating a less-discriminatory alternative exists.
If Mr. Macky's changes their interview process and the URM hiring rate increases to 4.8% (while White stays at 5.9%), does adverse impact still exist?
Impact ratio = 4.8% / 5.9% = 0.814. This is above the 0.80 threshold, so the 4/5ths rule would no longer flag adverse impact. However, best practice is to continually monitor and aim for equitable outcomes, not just passing the threshold.

Turnover Rate Formulas

Turnover is one of the most expensive HR problems. Understanding how to calculate and interpret it correctly is essential for workforce planning.

Basic Turnover Rate

Annual Turnover Rate Turnover Rate = (# Separations during period / Avg # of employees) × 100

Voluntary vs. Involuntary

TypeDefinitionDashboard LabelImplication
Voluntary (Quit)Employee chooses to leaveQuitsMay indicate dissatisfaction, better opportunities
Involuntary (Discharge)Employer terminatesDischargesPerformance issues, policy violations

The Cost of Turnover

Industry estimates suggest replacing an hourly employee costs $3,000–$5,000 per person (recruiting, training, lost productivity). For managers, this rises to 50–200% of annual salary. With Mr. Macky's seeing ~5,700 separations per year, even conservative estimates put annual turnover costs in the tens of millions.

📊 Dashboard Example The Flight Risk heatmap shows per-unit turnover rates. A unit with 90% turnover isn't just losing people — it likely has higher training costs, lower service quality, and reduced customer satisfaction. Cross-reference with the Restaurant Performance tab to see the financial impact.
Unit 42 had 80 total employees over the year, of which 60 separated (45 quit, 15 discharged) and 20 remain. What is the turnover rate? Is this primarily a "quit" problem or a "discharge" problem?
Turnover rate = 60/80 = 75%. It's primarily a quit problem — 45/60 = 75% of separations were voluntary. This suggests employees are choosing to leave, possibly due to management quality, pay, or working conditions — not performance terminations.

Selection Methods & Validity

Not all interview and selection methods are created equal. Understanding validity helps you evaluate whether your hiring tools actually predict job success.

Types of Validity

TypeWhat It MeasuresExample
Criterion ValidityDoes the test predict future job performance?Do interview scores predict post-hire performance ratings?
Content ValidityDoes the test sample the actual job content?A cooking test for a line cook position
Construct ValidityDoes the test measure the intended trait?Does a "leadership assessment" actually measure leadership?

Applying This to the Dashboard

Module 3 shows interview scores for hired vs. not-hired candidates. If hired candidates don't ultimately perform better (check Module 4), the interview may lack criterion validity — meaning it's not predicting what we want it to predict.

Module 1 shows which recruitment sources produce the best-performing managers. If Source A consistently produces higher performers than Source B, Source A has better predictive validity for our purposes.

💡 Key Insight The dashboard's "Experience-Performance Paradox" (Module 4) challenges a common assumption: that more experience = better performance. If prior experience doesn't predict performance ratings, then using experience as a major selection criterion has low validity — and we may be overpaying for a signal that doesn't predict outcomes.
Knowledge Check
1. A company uses a written test to select managers. Hired candidates score 80+ on the test but their job performance ratings are random (no relationship). What type of validity is lacking?
  • A Criterion validity
  • B Content validity
  • C Face validity
  • D Construct validity
✅ Correct! Criterion validity measures whether the test predicts future job performance. If high test scores don't correlate with high performance, the test lacks criterion validity.
❌ The answer is A. Criterion validity asks: does the test score predict the criterion (job performance)? If test scores don't correlate with performance, criterion validity is absent.
2. The dashboard shows that prior experience doesn't predict performance. If a company still uses "5 years minimum experience" as a hiring requirement, this is an example of:
  • A Good HR practice based on industry standards
  • B Content-valid selection
  • C Using a low-validity predictor that may also create adverse impact
  • D Construct-valid selection
✅ Correct! If experience doesn't predict performance, it's a low-validity predictor. Worse, experience requirements can disproportionately screen out younger candidates and certain demographic groups, potentially creating adverse impact.
❌ The answer is C. When data shows experience doesn't predict performance, using it as a requirement is a low-validity practice that may also create adverse impact.

✅ Correct! If outcomes (performance) consistently differ by source, the source itself becomes a valid predictor. This is a practical application of criterion validity.
❌ This is True. Predictive validity (a form of criterion validity) means the predictor (recruitment source) forecasts future outcomes (performance). Consistent differences confirm this.

Central Tendency & Distributions

The three "averages" — mean, median, and mode — each tell a different story about your data.

Mean, Median, and Mode

MeasureDefinitionWhen to Use
MeanSum of all values ÷ countSymmetric data, no extreme outliers
MedianMiddle value when sortedSkewed data, salary distributions
ModeMost frequently occurring valueCategorical data (e.g., most common job title)

Why This Matters for HR Data

Salary data is almost always right-skewed — a few highly-paid executives pull the mean upward. The median salary is usually a better representation of "typical" pay than the mean.

Performance ratings often cluster — many employees get a 3 (meets expectations), creating a strong mode. If the distribution is very peaked (low variance), the rating system may not be differentiating performers effectively.

📊 Dashboard Example The dashboard reports "Average Wage" in Module 4. When comparing demographics (male vs. female wages), ask: is the mean being pulled by a few outliers? If so, the median would be a more reliable comparison. The scatter plot lets you visually check for outliers.

Charts can inform or mislead. Here's how to critically evaluate the visualizations in the dashboard.

Chart Types in the Dashboard

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Ignoring the Y-axis scale — a bar chart that doesn't start at zero can exaggerate differences
  • Confusing area with value — in doughnut charts, thin slices may represent more than you think
  • Over-interpreting small samples — a trendline based on 3 data points is unreliable
  • Cherry-picking timeframes — always look at the full trend, not just favorable years
